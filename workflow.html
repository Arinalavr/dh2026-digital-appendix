Methodological appendix

AI-Assisted Qualitative Workflow

A transparent AI-assisted pipeline for working with small qualitative interview data.

This page documents the Stage 1 qualitative workflow behind the DH2026 project. AI tools supported transcription, candidate coding, synthesis scaffolding, and comparison, while final interpretation remained researcher-led and researcher-verified. The workflow stops here at Stage 1 findings and the selection of a concrete zone for deep interviews and 360° visualization.

Pipeline Overview

Field interviews
Local Whisper transcription
Speaker-labelled transcripts
Claude Opus 4.6 candidate coding
Researcher-directed consolidation
AFF / BEH / COG verification in Taguette
Voyant / Python visual checks
Stage 1 findings → Selected 360° zone

Deep interviews and AR scenario testing are ongoing and are not presented here as completed analysis.

Stage 1 Dataset

Stage 1 is based on 20 short on-site interviews collected around the abandoned hotel site. The interviews captured first reactions, beach use, affective responses, memories or contextual knowledge, and possible future imaginaries.

1. Local Transcription

Audio was transcribed locally using Whisper. For Stage 2 deep interviews, the project uses a reusable Python transcription script with regular openai-whisper, model medium, explicit language settings, and timestamped raw transcripts.

Raw transcripts are stored separately from later cleaned and speaker-labelled versions. This separation keeps the pipeline auditable: raw ASR output remains available, while interpretive editing and speaker labelling happen as later analytical steps.

  • openai-whisper
  • medium model
  • timestamped segments
  • metadata JSON
  • optional ffmpeg preprocessing

For noisy recordings, the workflow can optionally preprocess audio with ffmpeg: mono conversion, 16 kHz sample rate, loudness normalization, and light noise reduction.

2. Claude Opus 4.6 Candidate Coding

Claude Opus 4.6 was used for first-pass coding and code-structure scaffolding. It initially generated a larger candidate-code set. The researcher reviewed this output, decided it was too granular for the project, and directed consolidation into a smaller, thematically coherent code framework before manual verification.

108 candidate codes → researcher-directed consolidation → 37 first-pass codes → Taguette verification → 38 final researcher-verified tags

The AI-generated structure became a working layer for researcher judgement. The researcher evaluated the structure, requested reduction, then reassigned, revised, rejected, merged, and expanded codes during verification.

3. Affective / Behavioral / Cognitive Code Framework

The final coding was organized through an Affective / Behavioral / Cognitive framework. This framework was selected by the researcher to capture not only what respondents said about the site, but how they emotionally, practically, and interpretively related to it.

The framework draws on sense-of-place and place-attachment literature, especially the distinction between affective, behavioral, and cognitive dimensions of place experience (Konduri & Lee, 2023).

AFF

Emotions, atmospheres, aesthetic reactions, sadness, fascination, attachment, shame, or affect.

BEH

Practices and actions: walking, beach use, entering or not entering, exploration, and creative use.

COG

Interpretations, knowledge, imagined futures, governance critique, safety concerns, and reuse ideas.

Final Codebook

38 final tags:
- 10 AFF
- 11 BEH
- 17 COG

4. Researcher Verification In Taguette

I manually checked all codes in Taguette. Verification included recoding, merging, renaming, splitting categories, adding contextual codes, and rejecting interpretations that did not fit the data.

From cognition to behavior

COG.walking_trail was removed and merged into BEH.walking, because walking along the trail describes an action rather than a cognitive interpretation.

Adding double meaning

The quote "Let it be for students, but use it" was coded by the researcher as both COG.reuse_community_social and COG.wasted_potential.

Splitting visit frequency

BEH.visit_frequency was divided into BEH.visit_frequent, BEH.visit_occasional, and BEH.visit_first.

5. What The AI Missed And Where Human Context Was Added

The coding comparison showed a systematic pattern rather than random error. Claude Opus 4.6 reliably identified explicit statements but under-coded implicit and contextual references. My verification added contextual, indirect, and field-informed meanings.

The researcher identified 58% more tag assignments than the AI first-pass coding.

Researcher coding added more

  • COG.seasonality: +19
  • BEH.exploration: +13
  • BEH.first_discovery: +13
  • AFF.nature_beauty_appreciation: +11
  • AFF.graffiti_street_art: +9

Researcher coding reduced interpretation

  • AFF.nostalgia_former_glory: -5
  • COG.scale_architectural_critique: -4
  • AFF.identity_territorial_conflict: -3

This pattern is one reason the workflow is useful. AI can create a systematic first-pass baseline, and the researcher can then test where the baseline is too literal, too broad, or not contextual enough.

6. Disagreement As Analytical Checkpoint

Disagreement with AI outputs was treated as part of the analytical process. I first developed my own Stage 1 findings through close reading of coded materials, charts, word clouds, and field knowledge. AI-generated suggestions were then used as comparison material. Some suggestions were useful, some were reframed, and several were not taken into the final analysis.

One AI-generated interpretation suggested that entering the hotel itself changes perception. I rejected this causal framing. My interpretation was that prior attitudes toward ruins, risk, curiosity, and legitimacy shape whether people enter in the first place.

Agreement and disagreement with AI outputs were documented in Obsidian working notes and the AI-Assisted Research Audit Trail.

7. Visual Pattern Checks

After Taguette verification, exported highlights were examined through Voyant Tools and Python scripts. These visual checks helped compare code frequencies, respondent groups, and vocabulary patterns. They supported close reading by showing where patterns were concentrated or uneven.

Respondent-level heatmaps were used internally during analysis, but they are not published here because the working labels can expose interview-level metadata. The public appendix shows only aggregated visual outputs.

8. Researcher-Led Stage 1 Findings

  1. Nature is the main value that brings people to the site.
  2. The hotel is an affectively charged object, not a neutral background.
  3. For context-aware respondents, the hotel represents governance failure and wasted public potential.
  4. Non-entry is common and meaningful.
  5. Future imaginaries point toward mixed, small-scale, and non-monolithic reuse.

9. From Findings To Selected 360° Zone

The Stage 1 findings did not directly produce a final design solution. They helped narrow what should be explored next: a concrete zone for deeper interviews and 360° documentation.

  • visible from the beach path and parking area
  • accessible from the main movement route
  • possible to make safer with stairs and protective barriers
  • good views of the sea, beach, and mountains
  • existing graffiti and visual layers
  • connection to current beach use
